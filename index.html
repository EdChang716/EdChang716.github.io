Journal Submissions

Unpacking the Anna Karenina Principle: Mental Representations of Friendships and their Impact on Well-Being

Chang, C-Y. E., F-C B. Chou, W-T W. Lee, J-H Chang & P-H A. Chen. (In prep. for submission to Nature Communications)

Skills: Advanced Statistics Analysis, Mental Representations, Social Interactions, Mental Health


We utilized a novel approach to investigate the link between mental representations of friendships and well-being through the Anna Karenina principle. By employing representational analysis, we found strong evidence demonstrating that not only quality or quantity of friendships predict well-being, but also how individuals represent their relationships in their minds can determine their well-being. The paper is currently under preparation for submission to Nature Communication; therefore, further details cannot be disclosed at this time.

Leveraging Deep Learning and Imputation Techniques for Accurate Water Quality

Chang, C-Y. E., V. H-C Liao, F-J Chang. (Submitted to Science of the Total Environment)

Skills: Deep Learning, Statistics, Data Scraping, Environmental and Water Management


I presented a novel framework combining MICE-RF imputation and LSTM-ED models to predict Dissolved Oxygen (DO) and Ammonia Nitrogen (NH3-N) levels in the Dansuie River in Taipei. Leveraging over 20,000 hourly data points, our model significantly outperforms traditional LSTM and CNN-LSTM models, achieving up to 14.1% R² improvement for DO and 13.9% for NH3-N. It offers reliable multi-step forecasts and robust handling of missing data. The paper has been submitted to Science of the Total Environment and has not yet been published; therefore, further details cannot be disclosed at this time. .

Exploring cross-domain text semantic similarity using large language models

Chang, C-Y. E., Chou, F.-C. B., Chen, P.-H. A.(2023).
Taiwanese Psychological Association Annual Meeting

Skills: NLP, Large Language Model


We utilize OpenAI's text embedding model to investigate whether text embeddings accurately reflect semantic similarity and how the simialarities could be affected by the lengths of the texts from a dataset comprising nearly 35 articiles across various domains.
The results demonstrate that These embeddings successfully differentiate articles from various domains. However, longer articles demonstrated higher semantic similarity compared to shorter ones, even within the same domain. This project offers valuable insights for future researchers, emphasizing the need to account for article or conversational text length as a potential confounding factor in text similarity analysis using LLM methods.
